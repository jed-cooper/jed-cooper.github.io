DATA PROJECT 1

SUMMARY

For this analysis project I chose to explore the effect of audio streaming services on the music industry,
specifically their effect on the sales figures of physical albums.

The hypothesis I chose to test is: ‘streaming services have led to a decline in physical album sales.’


CONCLUSIONS

From examination of the figures extracted from the dataset, it can be inferred that the widespread implementation of streaming
services (including subscriptions to their services), from the period 2004-2005, led to a very sharp decline in the sales of physical albums.

Interpretation of the data preceeding this decline - the period 2003-2004 - indicates that there was indeed a recovery
in the overall sales of physical albums, triggered by an increase in the number of CD purchases (+20million units sold).

Remarkably, from 2007 onwards there has been a steady increase in the sales of physical LPs and EPs, with sales continuing to grow
even to this day. Also, from the period 2008-2019, the rate of decline in revenue generated by physical sales has slowed down dramatically.

CHALLENGES

Here I will briefly highlight a number of challenges I confronted during the pre-processing/cleaning of the data, and the process of visualisation.

Filtering:
In order to simplify the data and make sense of overall trends, it was necessary to drop columns of data from the original dataframe. I endevour as a software developer
to write succinct code, and I had intended to use the df.drop() function to remove all unnecessary columns using one line of code. For whatever reason
(I believe because of operator syntax) I could not get this function to operate on the dataset.

I did find a work-around using the df.pop() function, which is a simple procedure but meant putting the dataframe through the same
function multiple times (once for each column removed), which increased the time of development and perhaps the processing power needed to clean the data.

Indexing:
Prior to exporting the dataset before the visualisation stage, I needed to filter data by year to fit the parameters of the hypothesis (1994-2019). Up until this point,
the column denoting ‘Year’ and its data had not been operable as for some reason the program didn’t recognise the column ‘Year’, even though when I listed the columns
it was clearly displayed in the console.

I began troubleshooting by delimiting residual whitespace that may have been present, I tried reassigning the name of the column, and when that didn’t work I tried
exporting the file to excel, cleaning the data in the spreadsheet and re-exporting the file. This only worked the first time; when I tried it with the other dataframes,
it never worked again. Anyway after a couple of days of stressing about it, the program (for some esoteric reason..) began to recognise the column. Hooray!

X/Y-axis labelling
Using matplotlib to test out different visualisations was definitely my favourite part of the project, as it allowed me to finally see the hard-earned fruits of my data-scrubbing.
I had originally intended (and tried) to use the ‘Year’ column as the index for each dataframe, but there were prior issues and upon creating the line graph the labels on the
x-axis became skewed and overlapped, so I had to become familiar with the the plt.xtick() function of matplotlib.

To get the data displayed properly and fit the final graphic, I chose to pass an array of string values for each year to the xtick function, which placed the strings at
consistent intervals along the x-axis, and worked quite nicely: plt.xticks(range(len(years)), years, size='small').
